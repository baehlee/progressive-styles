Minneapolis, Minnesota

Living in Minneapolis.

Minneapolis is the bigger half of the Twin Cities — a city built around water, parks, and a creative streak that shows up in its food, music, and front porches. The Chain of Lakes (Bde Maka Ska, Lake Harriet, Lake of the Isles, and Cedar Lake) threads right through the southwest side, and the Grand Rounds Scenic Byway connects them by trail to the Mississippi riverfront. The park system is regularly ranked the best in the country, and the city is one of the most bike-friendly in the U.S. — which tells you a lot about how people here actually live.

For buyers, Minneapolis offers an unusually wide range: warehouse lofts downtown, character bungalows and historic homes near the lakes, and more attainable single-family neighborhoods across the north and south sides. Whether you're after walkability, a yard, or a short bike commute, there's a version of Minneapolis that fits.

What buyers should know.

The neighborhoods

Each pocket of the city has its own feel. North Loop and downtown are loft-and-restaurant territory for people who want to walk to everything. Northeast Minneapolis is the arts-and-breweries district, full of character homes and makers. The southwest lake neighborhoods — Linden Hills, Fulton, Lynnhurst, Lowry Hill, and the streets around Lake of the Isles — pair walkable village nodes with some of the city's most beautiful housing stock. South and Northside neighborhoods like Longfellow, Powderhorn, and Camden offer diverse communities and more attainable price points.

Getting around

The METRO Blue and Green light-rail lines connect the airport, downtown, the U of M, and St. Paul, backed by an extensive bus network. But the real story is the trails: you can get across much of the city on protected bike paths, and a lot of residents do, three seasons a year.

Parks, schools & everyday life

Minneapolis Public Schools serves the city alongside a wide mix of charter, magnet, and private options, so school fit is worth a real conversation rather than a single rating. And the parks aren't an afterthought — most homes are within a short walk of green space, a lake, or a trailhead, which shapes daily life here as much as any commute.

The housing stock

Minneapolis homes span more than a century of building. You'll find craftsman bungalows and Tudors from the 1910s–1930s across the south side, grand historic homes near the lakes and Lowry Hill, post-war ramblers in the outer rings, and a deep supply of condos and converted lofts downtown and in the North Loop. That range is the city's superpower for buyers: the same map holds a first condo, a forever home, and an investment duplex. It also means condition and updates vary widely from block to block — exactly where having an agent who knows what's behind the walls pays off.

Who Minneapolis fits

It's a strong match if you want walkability, water and trails out your door, a real arts-and-food scene, and a short commute. Families weigh school options carefully here; remote workers love the trail access and the price-to-space ratio compared with the coasts. If you'd trade a bigger lot for being able to walk or bike to your morning coffee, Minneapolis was probably built for you.
