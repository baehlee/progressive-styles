The process, step by step.

Here's the whole arc, start to finish. It's less intimidating once you can see all of it at once:

  • 1 · Get pre-approved. A lender reviews your finances and tells you what you can borrow. This comes first — it sets your budget and shows sellers you're serious.
  • 2 · Set your real budget. Beyond the price tag: taxes, insurance, and any association dues. We help you find the monthly number you're actually comfortable with.
  • 3 · Tour and find "the one." We'll line up showings that fit your list and your life, and give you an honest read on each home's condition and value.
  • 4 · Make an offer. We handle strategy, contingencies, and earnest money, and explain every term before you sign.
  • 5 · Inspect and finalize. Inspection, appraisal, and loan underwriting happen here. We help you negotiate repairs and keep deadlines from slipping.
  • 6 · Close and get your keys. You sign, funds transfer, and the home is yours. We're still here afterward for whatever comes next.

Mistakes we help you avoid.

Most first-time-buyer regrets trace back to a handful of avoidable missteps. We watch for all of them so you don't have to:

  • Skipping pre-approval. Shopping before you know your budget wastes time and weakens every offer you make.
  • Maxing out your budget. Being approved for a number doesn't mean you should spend it. We help you find a payment that leaves room to actually live.
  • Waiving the inspection. Tempting in a hot market, but it can hide expensive surprises. We help you weigh the real risk before you do.
  • Bidding with your heart. Falling for a house can lead to overpaying. We keep you grounded in what it's actually worth.
  • Forgetting closing costs. They're real and they add up. We make sure they're in your plan from day one, not a shock at the end.

Minnesota first-time buyer programs.

One of the most common myths is that you need 20% down. Many first-time buyers put down far less, and Minnesota has programs designed specifically to help.

Minnesota Housing (the state housing finance agency) offers first-time buyer loan programs and down-payment and closing-cost assistance for buyers who qualify. Eligibility, income limits, and the specific programs change over time, so the smart move is to talk it through early — we'll connect you with lenders who know these programs inside and out and help you see what you actually qualify for. There may also be city, county, and lender-specific options worth a look.

Questions we hear a lot

How much do I really need for a down payment?

Often a lot less than 20%. Depending on the loan and program, qualified buyers can put down considerably less, and Minnesota down-payment-assistance programs can help further. We'll get you a realistic number for your situation before you shop.

How long does buying a home take?

From going under contract to getting your keys is often around 30–60 days, plus however long the search itself takes. Being pre-approved and organized up front is the best way to keep everything moving smoothly.

What if my offer gets rejected?

It happens, especially in competitive markets — and it's not the end of the world. We learn from each round, sharpen the strategy, and keep going. The right home is worth a little patience.

Should I get pre-approved before I start looking?

Yes — it's step one. Pre-approval sets your budget, saves you from falling for homes out of range, and makes your offer credible when you find the right place. In a competitive market, sellers often won't take an offer seriously without it.

What credit score do I need?

There's no single magic number — different loan programs have different thresholds, and your rate is affected too. A lender can review where you stand, and if you're not quite there, we can point you toward steps to get ready.

What are closing costs, and who pays them?

Closing costs are the fees to finalize your loan and purchase — typically a few percent of the price. Sometimes they can be negotiated into the deal or covered by assistance programs. We'll make sure you see the full picture, not just the sticker price.

Is now a good time to buy my first home?

The honest answer is "it depends on you," not the headlines — your finances, how long you'll stay, and what's available in your range matter more than trying to time the market. We'll give you a straight assessment, not a sales pitch.
