Pre-qualification vs. pre-approval.

People mix these up constantly, and the difference matters:

  • Pre-qualification A quick, informal estimate based on numbers you tell a lender. Useful for a ballpark, but it carries little weight with sellers.
  • Pre-approval A lender actually reviews your finances and credit and issues a letter stating what they're prepared to lend. This is what makes your offer competitive — and what we'll want in hand before you shop seriously.

What you'll need.

Every lender is a little different, but a pre-approval generally involves documenting your income, assets, and credit. Having these ready makes it fast:

  • Proof of income Recent pay stubs, W-2s or 1099s, and often a couple years of tax returns — especially if you're self-employed.
  • Assets Bank and investment statements showing your down payment and reserves.
  • Identification & history ID, and a picture of your debts and employment history.
  • A credit review The lender will check your credit as part of approval. Shopping several mortgage lenders within a short window is generally treated as a single inquiry, so comparing offers won't pile up against you.

How much house can you afford?

Pre-approval tells you what a lender will lend — but "can borrow" and "should spend" aren't the same number. Your real budget includes more than principal and interest.

A monthly payment usually also covers property taxes, homeowners insurance, possibly mortgage insurance, and any association dues — and lenders weigh your existing debts through your debt-to-income ratio. We help you land on the payment that fits your life comfortably, not the maximum that fits the formula, so your home stays a foundation instead of a strain.

Why it makes your offer stronger.

When two similar offers land on a seller's table, the pre-approved buyer almost always wins — there's far less risk the deal falls apart at financing. Questions we hear a lot

Do I really need pre-approval to buy a house in Minnesota?

You're not legally required to have it, but practically speaking, yes — in a competitive market most sellers won't take an offer seriously without a pre-approval letter, and you won't know your true budget without one. It's the first step we recommend to every buyer.

What is PMI, and will I have to pay it?

Private mortgage insurance is often required when your down payment is below a certain threshold; it protects the lender, not you, and can usually be removed later as you build equity. Your lender will tell you whether it applies, and we'll make sure you understand the cost up front.

Does a pre-approval guarantee my loan?

Not quite — it's a strong, documented estimate, but final approval comes after full underwriting and the appraisal once you're under contract. That's why we help you avoid big financial moves — new debt, a job change — between pre-approval and closing.

Does getting pre-approved hurt my credit?

A pre-approval involves a credit check, which can have a small, temporary effect. The good news: credit scoring generally treats multiple mortgage inquiries within a short shopping window as one, so you can compare lenders without stacking up the impact.

How long does pre-approval take?

Often anywhere from a same-day quick turnaround to a few business days, depending on the lender and how ready your documents are. Having your paperwork together speeds it up a lot.

How long does a pre-approval last?

Typically around 60–90 days, since it's based on a current snapshot of your finances and credit. If your search runs longer, your lender can refresh it.

Are you the ones who approve my loan?

No — we're your real estate team, not a lender. But with our mortgage backgrounds, we'll help you understand the process, ask the right questions, and we'll connect you with trusted local lenders who do the approving.
