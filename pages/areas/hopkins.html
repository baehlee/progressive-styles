Hopkins · Twin Cities Metro

Living in Hopkins.

Hopkins is a small first-ring suburb with something most of its neighbors would love to have: a genuine, walkable historic downtown. Mainstreet Hopkins is lined with locally owned shops, restaurants, and the Hopkins Center for the Arts, and the annual Raspberry Festival still draws the whole community. For buyers, Hopkins offers small-town character and real value just minutes from Minneapolis.

It's also a city on the upswing: the METRO Green Line extension brings light-rail stations to Hopkins, adding transit-oriented options to its mix of classic older homes. For buyers priced out of pricier neighbors, Hopkins offers a rare combination — walkability, character, and a price point that still pencils — which is a big part of why we keep an eye on it.

What buyers should know.

The neighborhoods

Hopkins is compact and walkable, built around Mainstreet and its surrounding residential blocks of bungalows, ramblers, and modest mid-century homes. New transit-oriented development near the coming light-rail stations is adding condos and apartments to the mix.

Schools & getting around

Hopkins Public Schools — a district that reaches into several neighboring communities — serves the area. Highways 7 and 169 and I-394 keep commutes short, and the arriving METRO Green Line extension will connect Hopkins by rail to Minneapolis, St. Louis Park, and Eden Prairie.

Parks & everyday life

Walkability is the whole point here. You can live a car-light life around Mainstreet, with parks, the regional trail network, and the arts center close by. It's a rare metro suburb where "walk to dinner" is a real option.

The housing stock

Hopkins leans older and more affordable: 1920s–1950s bungalows and ramblers, plus newer transit-oriented condos near the rail stations. The older homes offer charm and value, with the usual older-home homework on roofs, foundations, and systems.

Who Hopkins fits

It's a strong match for first-time buyers, walkability lovers, and anyone betting on light rail — people who want character, value, and a real downtown over square footage and a big lot. Charming, central, and on the rise.

Why we are watching Hopkins

Hopkins is one of the more interesting value stories in the metro right now. It already has the thing money can't buy — a walkable, locally owned Mainstreet — and the arriving light-rail extension is adding transit-oriented housing and connectivity that tend to lift nearby values over time. For buyers, that's a chance to get in on character and walkability before the rail fully reshapes the area. For sellers, it's a story worth telling clearly: charm, value, and what's coming. We help on both sides read the older housing stock and the longer arc the trains are bringing.
