Wayzata · Twin Cities Metro

Living in Wayzata.

Wayzata is small in size and big in reputation — a lakefront city on the north shore of Lake Minnetonka with one of the most charming walkable downtowns in the metro. Boutiques, restaurants, and the historic depot line a waterfront that fills with boats all summer. For buyers, Wayzata is where lake lifestyle and small-town walkability meet at the top of the market.

Inventory here ranges from in-town condos and townhomes to some of Minnesota's most coveted lakefront estates, so the price spectrum is wide — but the lifestyle is the constant.

What buyers should know.

The neighborhoods

Downtown Wayzata offers low-maintenance condos and townhomes within walking distance of the lake, shops, and dining. Beyond the core, you'll find established homes on wooded lots and, along the shoreline, premium Lake Minnetonka properties that rank among the most sought-after in the state.

Schools & getting around

Wayzata anchors the highly regarded Wayzata Public Schools, whose reputation lifts demand well beyond the city limits. I-394 and Highway 12 give a clean, direct shot into Minneapolis, making the lake feel surprisingly close to downtown.

Parks & everyday life

Life here orbits the lake — the Wayzata Lake Walk, the beach, the boats, and a downtown calendar of events. It's a place where you can keep a boat, walk to dinner, and still be in the city in twenty-five minutes.

The housing stock

Expect a barbell: walkable downtown condos and townhomes at one end, and high-end estates — many on or near the water — at the other, with established mid-century and newer luxury homes in between. On lakefront in particular, shoreline, frontage, and condition drive enormous value differences.

Who Wayzata fits

It's a strong match for buyers who want lake living and a walkable downtown, downsizers seeking a low-maintenance condo near the water, and luxury buyers focused on Lake Minnetonka. If the lake is the dream, this is the address.

On the lake, details decide

Lake Minnetonka is the most detail-driven market in the metro. Two homes a hundred feet apart can differ wildly in value based on frontage, shoreline type, elevation, lake bottom, and even which bay they sit on. Association rules, dock rights, and flood considerations add more layers. For buyers, that complexity is exactly why local, on-the-water knowledge matters — it's easy to overpay for the wrong stretch of shore. For sellers, the same expertise helps you price and present a lake home to the narrow, motivated audience actually shopping for it. This is not a market to wing.
